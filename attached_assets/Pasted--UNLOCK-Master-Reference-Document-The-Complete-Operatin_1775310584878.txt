## Section 1 — Platform Overview

### What Unlock Is

Unlock is an AI-powered investment intelligence platform for self-certified sophisticated investors and high-net-worth individuals in the UK. It provides:

- **Portfolio stress-testing** — scenario analysis across 30+ macro events to surface hidden correlation risk
- **EIS/SEIS deal flow access** — curated pipeline of qualifying companies through Unlock Access
- **Decumulation sequencing** — intelligent drawdown planning that optimises the order in which assets are drawn to minimise tax and maximise estate value
- **Independent due diligence** — research on alternative investment opportunities that sit outside the scope of regulated advice

**What Unlock is not:** A financial adviser. An EIS fund. A regulated investment product. A platform that earns from the products it recommends.

**The structural moat:** Unlock earns from investors (platform subscription), not from the products investors buy. This structural independence means Unlock can provide genuinely neutral sequencing and portfolio advice that conflicted platforms — Hargreaves Lansdown, AJ Bell, Nutmeg — cannot.

**The platform evolution:** Unlock began as a due diligence platform. It has evolved into an asset registry with tools layered on top — driven by how founding investors actually use it. This evolution changes the nature of the platform: from episodic tool to persistent infrastructure. Each investor's portfolio data, tracked over time, builds the structured dataset that makes the platform more valuable for every investor on it.

⚠️ **Data governance requirement (CRITICAL — not yet documented):** The asset registry framing means Unlock stores real HNW portfolio data. Before using the data flywheel or co-creation claims in investor communications, an Investor Data Charter must be in place defining: data ownership (investor retains), usage (aggregated and anonymised only), opt-in consent, exit rights, and security standards. Without this, the data flywheel is a liability, not an asset.

## Section 2 — Strategy Foundation

### Core Narrative Spine

> **"The people who win in private markets are not those with access — but those with the infrastructure to make sense of it."**

Every communication, document, and conversation should be traceable back to this sentence.

### The Moat — Honest Assessment

Structural independence (earning from investors, not products) is the founding claim. It is a genuine and meaningful differentiator today. It is not a permanent moat.

A well-funded competitor could adopt a subscription model and neutral positioning — there is no technical or regulatory barrier preventing this. The real moat is built over time through: the data flywheel (each investor's portfolio data makes the platform more valuable for every other investor), trust relationships with sophisticated investors, and the founding cohort's active participation in product development.

**In investor communications:** Do not present structural independence as an unassailable moat. Present it as the structural condition that enables the data flywheel and trust network to build — those are the durable advantages.

**Approved framing:** "Structural independence is not the end state — it is the condition that allows everything else to develop without conflict."

### The Co-Pilot Positioning

Position Unlock as a co-pilot working alongside the investor — not a library waiting for them to do the work themselves. The platform does the heavy lifting. The investor retains control and makes the final call. This distinction matters: most investors want guided independence, not autonomy.

### The Three-Sentence Pitch

> "Most IFAs practically cannot advise on individual EIS companies — leaving sophisticated investors without structured support on the investments that could benefit them most. Unlock fills that gap with independent portfolio intelligence, deal flow access, and sequencing advice that no conflicted platform can provide. And right now, investing in Unlock itself — via EIS — means HMRC co-invests alongside you at 70p in the pound."

### What Makes Unlock Different From Incumbents

| Platform | Structure | What They Can't Do |
|---|---|---|
| Hargreaves Lansdown | Product distribution — earns from platform custody and fund charges | Cannot recommend moving assets off platform. Cannot provide neutral sequencing. Cannot route into direct EIS. |
| AJ Bell | Product distribution — similar model | Same as above |

### The Exit Thesis

The natural acquirer for Unlock is an established financial data or research business seeking private market intelligence at the individual investor level to complete their multi-asset coverage.

Private market data and research platform, founded 2007. Morningstar's rationale: "combining public and private market data to create comprehensive multi-asset intelligence." Now serves 100,000+ professionals globally. Source: Morningstar announcement, 14 October 2016. ✅ Confirmed

**2. Bureau van Dijk → Moody's, 2017, €3.0 billion**
European company information and private-company data platform. Moody's rationale: "extends Moody's position as a leader in risk data and analytical insight" and brings "one of the world's most extensive sources of private company information." Source: Moody's press release, 14 May 2017; completion announced 9 August 2017. ✅ Confirmed

Global financial markets data and trading platform (formerly Thomson Reuters Financial & Risk). LSEG rationale: "transform our position as a leading global Financial Markets Infrastructure group" — "Refinitiv brings highly complementary capabilities in data and capital markets." Source: LSEG press release, 31 July 2019; completion January 2021. ✅ Confirmed

**The pattern:** Neutral, independent data intelligence covering a market the acquirer wants → acquired at significant strategic premia by established financial data and infrastructure businesses (Morningstar, Moody's, LSEG).

**Unlock's position in this pattern:** Early-stage, pre-revenue, subscription-funded, structurally independent platform building individual investor portfolio intelligence. The natural exit path follows the same pattern — a financial data or infrastructure business acquiring the individual investor data layer to complete its multi-asset coverage.

**Approved language for investor communications:**

### EIS Portfolio Returns — After-Tax Framing

**THE CANONICAL RETURN FIGURE: 5-6x on effective cost**

This is the figure Unlock uses. It is honest, evidenced, and defensible. It must always be described as the return on effective cost — not as a gross return.

**The logic chain — locked in:**

| Step | Figure | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Gross investment | £100,000 | — |
| Income tax relief (30%) returned immediately | £30,000 | HMRC EIS — confirmed |
| Effective cost | £70,000 | Arithmetic |
| Portfolio return at model base case | £380,000 (3.8x gross) | Unlock model, calibrated to NESTA 2009 |
| All gains CGT-free | £380,000 fully retained | HMRC EIS CGT exemption — confirmed |
| Return on effective cost | **5.4x** | £380,000 / £70,000 — arithmetic |
| Presentation range | **5-6x** | Defensible range around the model base case |

**Approved call language — the canonical version:**

> "We target a 5-6x return on the money you actually put at risk. Here's how that works: income tax relief of 30% returns £30,000 immediately on a £100,000 investment — so your effective cost is £70,000. Our portfolio model produces a base case of 3.8x on the gross investment — that's £380,000 — all CGT-free. On the money you actually risked, that's 5.4x. We present it as 5-6x to reflect normal portfolio variance around the base case. Subject to individual tax circumstances."

**Why this is the right number to use:**

The investor never risked £100,000. They risked £70,000 — the rest came back immediately from HMRC. Quoting a gross multiple on the full investment understates the real economic return. 5-6x on effective cost is the correct representation of what the investor actually receives on what they actually deployed.

| Figure | What it is | Source |
|---|---|---|

## Section 5 — The Belief System

### Design Principles

Three types of investor state require three different approaches:

**Identity traits** — fixed characteristics. Analytical vs entrepreneurial. Self-directed vs adviser-led. Risk-averse disposition. Do not try to change identity traits. Work with them or disqualify. Never position Unlock as something that will change who someone is.

**Correctable beliefs** — things the investor believes that are factually wrong. "My IFA handles this." "EIS is too risky." "I missed the April 6 deadline." These change fastest. One good explanation is often enough.

**Developmental beliefs** — things the investor does not yet hold but can develop through the right content in the right sequence. The AI investment thesis. Portfolio intelligence value. The long-term BPR rolling programme. These take time and cannot be shortcut.

### Non-Negotiable Sequencing Rule

> **Explain EIS loss relief mechanics before asking about risk appetite.**

An investor who says "I'm risk averse" before understanding that their maximum effective loss is 38.5p per pound (EIS) or 27.5p per pound (SEIS) is responding to the wrong question. Establish U4 first. Then ask Q5. The answer will be materially different.

This rule applies in every call script, every document sequence, and every recommendation made by the tool.

## Section 12 — Objection Handling

### "My IFA handles this"

**Reality:** Most IFAs don't handle individual EIS. They handle EIS funds and VCTs.
**Response:** "Your IFA is probably great with ISAs, pensions, and fund-based EIS — that's their bread and butter. Direct EIS company shares sit outside what most adviser networks allow them to advise on. That's exactly why Unlock exists — to fill the gap that advisers can't."

---

### "EIS is too risky"

**Reality:** They are assessing risk against the gross investment, not the net exposure after reliefs.
**Response:** Establish U4 first. "Let me show you what the actual downside looks like after income tax relief and loss relief. For an additional rate taxpayer, the maximum effective loss on a total failure is approximately 38.5p per pound. That changes the risk calculation significantly."

**Never say:** "EIS is low risk." Capital is at risk. The point is that the downside is smaller than they think — not that there is no downside.

---

### "I'll speak to my accountant first"

**Response:** "Absolutely — that's exactly the right instinct. We can send your accountant a briefing note that covers the EIS mechanics, the BPR qualifying clock, and the January 2027 carry-back structure. It's designed specifically for advisers who need the detail quickly."
→ Trigger: send document 241 (Adviser Briefing Note) when it exists.

---

### "I missed the April 6 deadline"

**Reality:** They haven't missed anything. The new tax year just opened.
**Response:** "You haven't missed anything — you've actually got a better opportunity now. Both the 2025/26 and 2026/27 tax years are open simultaneously. Your January 2027 Self Assessment bill — the one for 2025/26 — is due in nine months and you haven't paid it yet. An investment today can reduce that bill directly."

---

### "Private companies just lag public markets — they're not really uncorrelated"

**Reality:** This is partially correct and should be acknowledged.
**Response:** "You're right that economic linkage remains — EIS portfolios aren't immune to recessions. What changes is the mechanism. Private companies aren't priced daily, so you don't experience the same daily sentiment swings. And the outcomes are driven by company execution over time, not by what the FTSE does on a Tuesday. The more important point is that in a correction, the demand for intelligent portfolio stress-testing goes up — because the consequences of getting sequencing wrong become visible."

---

### "Why would I invest in Unlock instead of an EIS fund?"

**Two angles:**

1. **As an investment:** You're backing an AI platform at founding round terms (£6.5M pre-money, anti-dilution, pro-rata) with EIS reducing your entry cost to 70p in the pound. The natural acquirers for this category have paid significant multiples — PitchBook sold to Morningstar for $225M, Bureau van Dijk sold to Moody's for €3bn, Refinitiv sold to LSEG for $27bn. All three followed the same pattern: neutral independent data intelligence acquired at strategic premium.

2. **As a portfolio strategy:** Direct EIS via Unlock Access is also available alongside the Unlock investment itself. EIS funds charge 8-14% in fees over a typical hold. Unlock replaces that fee layer with a subscription. The fee drag compounds — and you keep the difference.

---

### "The valuation seems high for a pre-revenue business"

**Response:** "The £6.5M pre-money is pricing the data asset and the infrastructure — not current revenue. And this isn't a pre-product business. Five founding investors — including senior figures from Barclays Stockbroking and TISA — are actively using the platform and feeding their experience back into the product. The comparable is PitchBook at founding stage, not a SaaS business at Series A. The terms reflect execution risk: anti-dilution to Series A means you're protected if the path is harder than expected. But the validation already in place — senior people using the tools and shaping what gets built — is exactly the signal you want at this stage."

**The co-creation point is critical here.** It answers "pre-revenue" by reframing what the founding round actually is: not a bet on a concept, but participation in a product being built with the people it serves.

### Document Usage Matrix

The matrix is encoded in `api-server/src/data/document-usage-matrix.ts` and contains three layers:

1. **DOCUMENT_RULES** — per-document metadata, worth-it rating (1-3 stars), positive triggers, hard exclusions ("don't send when"), prerequisites, never-send-simultaneously pairs, persona-never-first rules

2. **PIPELINE_SEQUENCE** — ordered send rules per pipeline stage with explicit exclusion rules

3. **PERSONA_ROUTING** — archetype-specific core sequences and never-first rules

The `shouldExclude()` function runs as a pre-filter in the rank endpoint before Claude scores relevance. Hard rules are enforced deterministically — Claude only scores documents that survive the matrix filter.

### Matrix Context Flags

Three boolean flags are auto-derived from transcript analysis and passed to the rank endpoint:

| Flag | Derived From | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| `eis_familiar` | Absence of EIS education gaps in analysis | If true: doc 150 (Secret Weapon) excluded from recommendations |
| `iht_confirmed` | IHT keywords in transcript summary (with negation handling) | If false: doc 170 (IHT planning) excluded |
| `adviser_mentioned` | Adviser references in objections or summary (with negation handling) | If true: doc 241 (Adviser Note) surfaces |

Manual override available in the Context Signals panel in the UI. Toggle re-runs rank immediately.

### QC Engine

19 mandatory checks run on every generated document. Chunking applied for documents >15,000 characters. False positives auto-resolved (auto_resolved_count tracked). The 19 checks include:

- Compliance figure verification (BPR cap, VCT rate, loss relief figures)
- Approved/prohibited language checks
- Decumulation Planner status phrase check
- Minimum investment check (£40,000)
- Capital at risk declaration
- "Subject to" qualifier checks

### Worth-It Weighting

Documents have a worth_it rating (★=1, ★★=2, ★★★=3) from the document usage matrix. This is passed to Claude's ranking prompt as a base weight. Higher worth-it documents are sorted first before conflict resolution, so ★★★ documents survive never-simultaneously exclusion over ★★ documents.

### Excluded Documents Response

The rank endpoint returns `excluded_documents[]` with per-document reasons. The frontend shows these in a collapsible "Filtered by Matrix" panel in the recommendations view.

### Build Item 2 — Change Taxonomy for Feature Update Cascade

**What:** Add a change_class field to every feature update submission.
The propagation engine uses the change class to determine the
appropriate action per dependency — not a blanket cascade.

**Why:** Currently the feature update cascade treats all changes the
same way. A typo correction and a regulatory figure change both
trigger the same response. The change taxonomy means the urgency
and required action differ based on what actually changed — which
is both more accurate and less operationally disruptive.

**The five change classes:**

| Class | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| cosmetic | No meaning change — typo, punctuation, spacing, formatting | Fix "it's" → "its" in a document |
| editorial | Meaning preserved, wording improved | Clearer phrasing of the advice gap explanation |
| semantic | Meaning materially changes — claim narrowed, caveat added | "cannot advise" → "practically cannot advise" |
| regulatory | Compliance basis changes — tax rate, law change, key value | 22p → 27.5p SEIS loss relief figure |
| structural | Composition logic changes — section added, sequence reordered | Adding F0 belief before founding round section |

**Propagation actions by change class and dependency type:**

| Change Class | Hard Dependency | Soft Dependency | Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| cosmetic | auto_update | log only | log only |
| editorial | review_required | log only | log only |
| semantic | review_required | review_recommended | log only |
| regulatory | approval_required | review_required | log only |
| structural | review_required | log only | log only |

**Immediate application:**

The two cascades ready to run now should be classified:
- Loss relief figure correction (22p → 27.5p): **regulatory** →
  approval_required on all hard dependents
- April 6 → January 2027 framing: **semantic** → review_required
  on all hard dependents

**UX change:** When Tom submits a feature update, the UI must ask
him to select the change class before submission. The change class

## Section 16c — Conversation Playbook (TO BUILD)

### What This Is

A structured but flexible conversation framework for use across
all investor touchpoints — cold call, follow-up, compliance call,
demo, and post-demo. Not a script. A set of questions the agent
genuinely wants answers to, in a sequence that feels natural to
the investor.

The playbook can run across one call or multiple calls depending
on how the conversation flows. The sequence is fixed; the pace
is set by the investor.

---

### The Four Question Types Required

**1. Qualifying Questions**
Establish fit. Binary routing — does this person qualify or not.
Must feel like natural conversation, not interrogation.
Frame as: "Just so I send you the right thing..."

Key qualifiers:
- Pays higher or additional rate income tax
- Can deploy £40,000+ in the next 12 months
- Makes own investment decisions (or adviser who will engage)

---

**2. Profiling Questions**
Build the picture. No right or wrong answer — every answer routes
the conversation. These determine which cluster the investor
belongs to and which SPIN sequences are relevant.

Areas to profile:
- Current investment activity and asset types
- Whether they work with an IFA/accountant and how
- EIS/SEIS familiarity and prior experience
- Broad financial priorities (growing / protecting / passing on)
- Estate size signals (without asking directly)
- Pension situation and awareness of April 2027 change
- Tax position and annual liability

---

**3. Hot Button Questions**
Find the real reason. Not the financial goal — the life goal that
requires the financial goal.

Hot buttons for Unlock investors:
- Family security: "I want to know my kids and grandkids are
  taken care of whatever happens"
- Freedom: "I want to retire when I want, not when I can afford to"
- Legacy: "I've built something — I want to pass it on, not hand
  half of it to HMRC"
- Relief: "I want to stop losing money to tax every year and
  feeling like I can't do anything about it"
- Significance: "I want to back things that matter, not just
  park money in funds"

The hot button question must be oblique — asking about life, not
money. It surfaces the real motivation in the investor's own words.
Once found, every subsequent question, document, and follow-up
is anchored to their specific hot button — not to EIS mechanics.

---

**4. SPIN Questions**
Surface and develop the problem. Situation → Problem →
Implication → Need-Payoff.

Six SPIN sequences — one per core problem Unlock solves.
Deploy based on what profiling reveals. Never run all six.

The six problems:
1. The advice gap — no structured support for direct EIS decisions
2. Portfolio correlation risk — hidden concentration not yet modelled
3. The January 2027 tax bill — a liability they can address now
4. The broken estate plan — April 2027 pension IHT change
5. The fee drag — EIS funds taking 8-14% that compounds against returns
6. The infrastructure gap — direct EIS without tools is unmanageable

Key Need-Payoff question (works across all problems):
"If instead of paying £20,000 of income tax each year, you turned
that into £100,000 tax free — how would that change the way you
look at tax? What impact would that have on your [hot button]?"

The £20K→£100K example should be personalised to the hot button
already identified. Not "your goals" — "your retirement" or
"what you leave your kids" or "your daughter's wedding."

---

### Structure of the Full Playbook

The playbook is a single flowing sequence with natural break points.
The agent goes as far as the conversation allows.

**Break Point 1** — after qualifying questions
→ Confirm fit. If no: exit gracefully. If yes: continue.
→ Natural pause: "Let me send you something to look at."

**Break Point 2** — after profiling questions
→ Persona and cluster identified. Hot button detected.
→ Natural pause: "Based on what you've told me, I know exactly
  what to show you. Let me send you [specific document]."

**Break Point 3** — after SPIN sequences
→ Problem and implication developed. Need-Payoff delivered.
→ Natural pause: "It sounds like this is worth a proper
  conversation. Can we book 30 minutes?"

**Break Point 4** — post-demo
→ Objection handling. What's in the way.
→ Natural close or next step confirmed.

---

### Design Principles

- Questions should sound like things Tom would actually say
- Every question should feel easy and low-stakes to answer
- The investor should feel understood, not assessed
- No question should feel like it has a right or wrong answer
- The hot button is never asked for directly — it surfaces through
  adjacent questions about life, not money
- Risk disposition is never asked before loss relief mechanics
  are explained — this is non-negotiable

---

### The Combined Methodology

Three frameworks combined into one conversation architecture.
Not used separately — layered in sequence.

**Layer 1 — Tactical Empathy (Chris Voss)**
Opens the conversation and builds trust before anything else.
Used throughout — not just at the start.

Key techniques:
- **Mirroring:** Repeat the last 2-3 words with an upward inflection. Invites the investor to continue without asking a direct question.
- **Labelling:** "It sounds like..." / "It seems like..." — name what you're picking up. Never "I feel like you're..." Always "It sounds like..."
- **Calibrated questions:** Always "How" or "What" — never "Can you" or "Will you." Calibrated questions are open, can't be answered yes or no, and make the investor feel in control.
- **No-oriented questions:** Frame questions so the investor can say no and still move forward. "Would it be a problem if I sent you something to look at?" is easier to say yes to than "Can I send you something?" The no feels safe — and safe is where sophisticated investors make decisions.
- **Silence:** Ask and stop talking. Let the investor fill the space. Talking through the pause is the most common mistake.

**Layer 2 — SPIN Selling**
Uncovers the problem and develops its implications before offering any solution. The investor sells themselves.

- **Situation:** Understand where they are now. Facts, context, current setup. Keep brief.
- **Problem:** Surface the dissatisfaction or gap. Not "do you have a problem" — "how do you currently handle X?"
- **Implication:** Develop the consequences of the problem. This creates urgency from within — not manufactured. "What happens to your January bill if you don't do anything before you file?"
- **Need-Payoff:** Help the investor articulate the value of the solution in their own words. "What would it mean for you if you could reduce that bill without touching your pension?"

**Layer 3 — Straight Line System (Belfort)**
Controls momentum once trust and need are established.

- **The Three Certainties:** Before any close, the investor must believe in three things: (1) EIS/the product itself, (2) Unlock as the right platform, (3) Tom as the right person to trust. Pitching before all three are established wastes the pitch.
- **Future pacing:** "Imagine it's January 2027 and your bill has already been reduced by the EIS relief — that's what we're building toward today."
- **Looping:** When an objection surfaces, don't argue — acknowledge, reconnect to something they've already agreed with, then address.

**The sequence for a full Unlock conversation:**

Empathy (Voss) → Situation (SPIN) → Problem (SPIN) → Implication (SPIN) → Certainty check → Pitch → Need-Payoff (SPIN) → Close (Straight Line)
